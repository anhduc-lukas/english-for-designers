# I Used to Need a Team

*Storytelling talk — English for Designers, May 2026*
*Lukáš Ngo, Popcorn Group*

---

## Hook

Last year, I asked an AI to help me write a document.

I expected plain text. Maybe some bullet points.

Instead, I got a fully styled Word document. Correct fonts. Correct margins. Proper headings. Ready to send.

And I just sat there for a second.

Because that was the moment I realized — something is shifting. Not what a designer *does*. But what a single designer can *do alone.*

---

## Who I Am

My name is Lukáš. I run a small creative studio — branding, web, UX, photo, video.

I also have restaurant projects on the side.

One person. A lot of hats.

And for a long time, that meant constant decisions about what I *couldn't* do — because I didn't have the time, the budget, or the right person.

---

## Before

A year ago, this is what my limitations looked like:

- Need business analysis for a restaurant? Hire a consultant.
- Client email that needs to sound professional? That's an hour of my evening.
- Food photos that need retouching? Find an editor.
- Website bug at 11pm? Hope your developer picks up.

Not impossible. Just *slow.* Just *expensive.* Just enough friction to make you skip it.

---

## The Moment

That Word document wasn't impressive because AI "can do things."

It was impressive because it removed friction between the idea and the output.

And when friction disappears — you think differently.

You stop asking *"is this worth my time?"*

You start asking *"what do I actually want to make?"*

That's a completely different question.

---

## After

Now I do things I simply didn't do before. Not because I couldn't — but because it wasn't worth the cost.

- Restaurant management advice? I work it through with AI before any meeting.
- Client emails? Five minutes, done.
- Food photography? Pre-generated, retouched, ready.
- Web support at midnight? Handled.

One person. Output of a small studio.

---

## The Big Idea

AI is not a tool.

It's a multiplier.

And the designers who understand that first — not because they know how to prompt, but because they know *what they want* — those are the ones who win.

The skill isn't technical. The skill is clarity.

---

## Takeaway

So I'll leave you with one question. Not *"will AI replace me?"*

That's the wrong question.

The right question is:

> "What would you make — if execution was free?"

Because right now, for the first time, it almost is.
